The Root Note

Every chord begins with a single note — the root. It gives the chord its name and its center of gravity. All other notes are measured from here.

02

Chord Inversions

The same notes, rearranged. Inversions change the lowest note of a chord — shifting its weight and color without changing its identity.

Inversions

Same chord.
Different feel.

Take C major: C – E – G. Move the C to the top and you get the 1st inversion (E – G – C). Move E to the top for the 2nd. Every version contains the same three notes — but each one lands differently under the fingers, and differently on the ear.

In any major key, each chord has a number. Those numbers tell you its role — its tension, its resolution, its drama. Change the key; the numbers move with you.

04

The Circle
of Fifths

Twelve keys arranged so that each neighbor is a perfect fifth apart. It's the map every composer keeps in their head.

Circle of Fifths

Every key,
connected

Move clockwise and you add one sharp. Move counter-clockwise and you add one flat. Adjacent keys share six of their seven notes — which is why chord progressions that travel along the circle feel so natural to the ear.

Pro tip: The V chord of any key is always the next key clockwise. That's why V→I is the most powerful resolution in music — you're traveling one step back home.
